Question Formats

WHAT QUESTION FORMATS ARE THERE?

Scale Question

This is useful for conducting Net Promoter Score analysis. With this question format, you can ask a user to rate something, for example, "On a scale of 1 to 10, how easy was our checkout process?" The responses to this question will definitely help you determine if your checkout or on-boarding experience need usability improvements.

Learn More

Open Response Question

You can use this type of questions to ask your users open-ended questions, and allow them to type in their responses. These questions are VERY valuable for generating insights to help you understand what's preventing your users from buying (objections), or what benefits they are experiencing from your product/service. These questions can also help you discover possible usability issue or broken things on your site by asking "is there anything on this site that's not working according to your expectation?"

Learn More

Emotion Response Question

This is an easy, no friction way to gauge how your user feels about their experience on your site. This type of questions will help you figure out whether a certain part of your site frustrates or delights them, or if they don't really care about it at all. From the insight you get here, you can craft your next micro survey to more deeply explore issues that are either frustrate or delight users. For example if you suspect users are having a hard time finding the right product after searching on an ecommerce store, you can use this question format to learn how the user feels about the search results they saw. Over time, you can use this data to improve products listed in search results.

Learn More

Multiple Choice Question

Ask your users multiple-choice questions to learn what benefits of your product/service they think are most important, and a slew of other things. Multiple-choice questions are very useful to "quantify" what you've discovered in open response format questions. If your users give open format responses that fall into roughly 4 categories, you can run a follow up micro-survey with a multiple choice question based on what you've learned. Imagine how powerful this is when you're asking about benefits you can communicate on your site, and you're wondering what order you should communicate those benefits in. You simply allow your users to "vote" and then make sure to mention these most popular benefits in your headline. That's data-driven copywriting.

What are Micro-surveys?

Micro-surveys are short and to the point. They only have one or two questions and they’re extremely targeted. This means that they’re not only easy to answer, but easy to create, implement, and analyze.

Imagine a micro-survey that asks “What prompted you to leave the registration page?” --- sent to a user that just left the registration page. Now compare it to a broader, more traditional survey that asks questions like “What is unappealing about our service?” several weeks after the user had an interaction with your website. General questions, sent after the fact, are difficult to answer. Users may be more likely to answer one quick question in-context, while on your site, than a lengthy list of questions a week after their visit.

Micro-surveys are a win/win: You spend less time creating and analyzing surveys, and users are more likely to respond than to a traditional survey.
